International Menstruation Day

Anne Mugo

March 8, 2022

My mother once had told me that when she was starting periods, she was clueless that this would happen. Nobody had told her about it. She was shocked and thought, she might have a horrible disease. That was the early 1950s in Germany.

A coincidence brought me to a panel discussion for the “World Menstrual Hygiene Day 2019” held in Berlin. Since 2014, every year 28th May is dedicated to raising awareness of menstruation issues and challenges facing many women and gilrs. Well, it was as incidental as many things happening to us. I had met a young woman in Kenya -Lucy Wanjiku- who founded Positive Young Women Voices, an organisation working to bring more awareness about the issue in Dandora. In Dandora, as in many other places, many girls and young women cannot attend school during their period, because they are too poor to afford sanitary towels. I was not aware of that problem and as I am a journalist, I quite immediately thought about doing a report about the work of that Kenyan woman and about the issue in general.

Many might question: “How can this guy not know about an ubiquitous topic like that, a topic that concerns directly half of the world population every month?” Well, think about, what you know about the weird male mind!

Nevertheless, when I told female friends about my assignment, they reacted astonished, some with disbelief. “Why will you be on that panel?” some said. “What do you know about menstruation? You are a man!”

I had to laugh a bit and explained the reason, why I was quite fitting in a discussion group about menstruation. Because, when you think about it, you find out that it is an issue that reflects, what is still not right in the relation between many men and women. The taboo around menstruation, the reluctance and ignorance many men show, when it comes to this issue, is an example of the inequality between genders. This inequality is the basis of a lot of harm, from unequal opportunities to sexual violence to femicide. A society, which believes in the fundamental right of equality between all human beings should be aware of these issues and talk openly about them.

Some men (and women) might react with a certain insecurity, even fear, when it comes to talking about menstruation. If a taboo or a special treatment is put on a fact of life, it influences the thinking and feeling of everyone. Nevertheless, menstruation is a natural process, which deserves our knowledge and respect. It is a topic that should be talked about and taught to everyone, young and old, to dissolve a taboo into something normal. If you research about menstruation, wherever you live, you find out that in a lot of cultures it is linked to impurity, uncleanliness, even a certain danger. This is true for Western societies and for many Arab, Asian, African and South American societies. Most religions see menstruation as either a holy process or a process, which leads to the exclusion of women from religious practices and society for the time of their menstruation. Bloody hell! Isn’t that awkward? Something that is essential for the human cycle of life, without which no life would be (and not yours, fellow reader), is seen as something that has to be excluded or pushed out of our attention? If a woman does not want to talk about the issue with men, I fully understand. As most men (including me) have no clue about the pain and hormonal turmoil that can come with menstruation. Men don’t know about it as they never experienced it. Wouldn’t it be nice, if men developed some basic understanding without referring to simple reactions like “she is moody, she must have her period?”

That is the more harmless, yet silly reaction. But if a society decided to exclude women from daily life, because of menstruation, this seems to overstep any idea of human equality and respect. If you research a bit, you find the idea that menstruation is, in some traditional societies, a phases, in which women are seen as something special. It is linked to a status of holiness. This was apparently turned around by most religions like Hinduism, Judaism, Christianism and Islam to the opposite, introducing the notion of impurity into menstruation. Women in their period should not go to the temple, church or mosque and were excluded from the social community for that time. I would understand and respect, if women decide for themselves that they want to be left in peace during that time due to the pain they suffer. But that is totally up for them to decide. Exclusion leads to a special treatment which can lead to a special kind of oppression – like female mutilation, which is a very sinister part of oppression of women. So, men have no say about that and should rather listen to women and understand, what they want.

The taboo around menstruation leads to oppression of women and to the oppression of nature, which some societies are quite successful in. We should fully embrace nature and accept it, as long as it is not harmful to anyone. Those, who believe in inequality of men and women, might interpret menstruation as a proof to it. Those who don’t should reconsider their take on the issue. I believe in equality, as it is in my view the best way for peaceful, prosperous and happy societies. Therefore, every woman should get everything she needs to be an equal part of everyday life, also when they have their menstruation. Sanitary towels should be available everywhere and the lack of it should become a thing of the past. If women decide to take a time off during their menstruation, they should have the right to do so. And no man should perceive a woman as being weak during that time. Remember that nobody would live without a mother. Without a woman with menstruation. So, let’s fight against ignorance and exclusion. It is a fight against violence and oppression, a fight for a better world.

By Thomas Hasel
Berlin

(Thomas has raised more than Ksh. 10,000 for our Project Adopt A Girl’s Month and he is still at it. Thank you Thomas.}
